Houston has more roofing contractors than almost any city in the country — and storm season makes that number surge overnight. After every major hailstorm or hurricane, hundreds of out-of-state crews roll into the metro, knock on doors, and offer to get you a new roof "through your insurance." Some are legitimate. Many are not.

Choosing the wrong contractor doesn't just mean a bad roof. It can mean a botched insurance claim, materials that fail in two years, or a lien placed on your home if the contractor skips paying their subcontractors. Here's exactly what to check before you sign anything.

7 Things to Check Before Hiring a Roofer in Houston

  1. Texas registration and local permits
    Texas does not require a statewide license for residential roofing, but the City of Houston requires contractors to register with the city and pull permits for most roofing jobs. Ask whether the contractor will pull a permit for your project — a legitimate operation will say yes without hesitation. If they suggest skipping the permit to "save you money," that's a warning sign.
  2. General liability insurance + workers' compensation
    Ask for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) before anyone gets on your roof. The COI should show general liability coverage (minimum $1 million per occurrence is standard) and workers' comp. Call the insurance carrier directly to verify the policy is active. If a worker is hurt on your property and the contractor doesn't have workers' comp, you could be held liable.
  3. BBB accreditation and rating
    Check the contractor's rating at bbb.org. Look for an A or A+ rating with zero unresolved complaints. Pay attention to the nature of any complaints — a single complaint from years ago resolved in good faith is very different from three open complaints about incomplete work or no-shows.
  4. Local references in your neighborhood or city
    Ask for references from jobs completed in the Houston area within the past 12 months. A contractor who does regular work here will have no trouble providing three to five names you can call. Ask those homeowners whether the crew showed up on schedule, cleaned up after themselves, and whether there have been any issues with the work since completion.
  5. Written contract with material specs, timeline, and warranty
    Before any work begins, you should have a signed contract that specifies the shingle brand and product line, the underlayment, the number of squares, the start and expected completion date, the payment schedule, and the warranty — both the manufacturer's warranty and the contractor's workmanship warranty. Verbal agreements are worth nothing in Texas courts.
  6. No same-day pressure to sign
    Storm chasers create urgency: "I have one crew available this week," "prices go up after storm season," "I can only lock in this price today." A legitimate local contractor will give you time to review the contract, check their credentials, and compare estimates. If someone is pressuring you to sign on the spot, walk away.
  7. A physical local address — not a PO box or 1-800 number
    Look up the business address on Google Maps. Does a real office exist there? Does their website list a local Houston-area phone number? Out-of-state storm chasers often operate with a temporary local phone number and a PO box. If you can't find a verifiable local business presence, keep looking.

Red Flags to Avoid

Red Flag #1: Offering to "waive your deductible" or "work it into the claim."
Under Texas Insurance Code Section 707.002, it is a criminal offense for a contractor to offer to pay, absorb, or rebate your insurance deductible. This is insurance fraud — and if you participate knowingly, you could face legal consequences too. If any roofer makes this offer, end the conversation immediately.

Red Flag #2: Door-knocking only after storms with no verifiable business presence.
Storm chasers specifically target neighborhoods that were hit. There's nothing wrong with a contractor introducing themselves after a storm, but if they can't show you a local address, a current COI, and local references within 24 hours of asking, they're not a company you want doing tens of thousands of dollars of work on your home.

Red Flag #3: Vague estimates with no material specifications.
If an estimate says "roof replacement — $12,000" with no mention of the brand, shingle line, wind rating, or warranty, you have no way to compare it to other quotes or verify that you received what you paid for. A professional estimate should read like a detailed scope of work, not a napkin figure.

Questions to Ask Before Signing

Before you sign any roofing contract in Houston, get clear answers to these five questions:

  • "Will you pull a permit for this job?" — The correct answer is yes.
  • "Can I see a current Certificate of Insurance?" — They should hand it over without hesitation and let you call the insurer.
  • "Who will be on my roof — your employees or subcontractors?" — Subcontractors aren't necessarily bad, but you should know, and the contractor should be able to confirm they're covered under the same insurance.
  • "What shingle product and wind rating are you using?" — In Houston, you want a minimum 130 mph wind rating; Class 4 impact resistance is ideal for hail coverage discounts.
  • "What does your workmanship warranty cover and for how long?" — Manufacturer warranties cover materials; workmanship warranties cover installation errors. Look for at least 2 years on workmanship; 5 years is better.

An out-of-state crew has no such incentive.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a roofing contractor in Texas need to be licensed?

Texas does not require a statewide contractor license for most residential roofing work, but contractors must comply with local city and county permit and registration requirements. Some cities in the Houston area require contractors to register locally. Always ask for proof of general liability insurance and workers' compensation regardless of licensing status.

Is it illegal for a roofer to offer to waive my deductible in Texas?

Yes. Under Texas Insurance Code Section 707.002, it is illegal for a contractor to pay, waive, or rebate an insurance deductible. If a roofer offers to "cover your deductible" or "work it into the insurance claim," that is insurance fraud. Walk away immediately.

How do I verify a roofing contractor's insurance in Houston?

Ask the contractor to provide a Certificate of Insurance (COI). Call the insurance carrier listed on the certificate to verify the policy is active and covers the scope of your job. A legitimate contractor will have no problem providing this before any work begins.

7 Cosas que Verificar Antes de Contratar a un Roofer en Houston

  1. Registro en Texas y permisos locales
    Texas no requiere una licencia estatal para trabajo de techado residencial, pero la Ciudad de Houston requiere que los contratistas se registren y obtengan permisos para la mayoría de los trabajos de techo. Pregunta si el contratista va a obtener un permiso para tu proyecto. Un negocio legítimo dirá que sí sin dudar.
  2. Seguro de responsabilidad general + compensación laboral
    Pide un Certificado de Seguro (COI) antes de que alguien suba a tu techo. El COI debe mostrar cobertura de responsabilidad civil (mínimo $1 millón por ocurrencia) y compensación laboral. Llama directamente a la aseguradora para verificar que la póliza está vigente.
  3. Acreditación y calificación BBB
    Verifica la calificación del contratista en bbb.org. Busca una calificación A o A+ sin quejas sin resolver. Una empresa seria en Houston tendrá historial verificable.
  4. Referencias locales en tu vecindario o ciudad
    Pide referencias de trabajos completados en el área de Houston en los últimos 12 meses. Un contratista que trabaja regularmente aquí no tendrá problema en dar tres a cinco nombres a los que puedas llamar.
  5. Contrato escrito con especificaciones de materiales, fechas y garantía
    Antes de que empiece cualquier trabajo, debes tener un contrato firmado que especifique la marca de las tejas, el fieltro, el número de cuadros, la fecha de inicio y fin, el calendario de pagos y la garantía tanto del fabricante como del contratista.
  6. Sin presión para firmar el mismo día
    Los "storm chasers" crean urgencia artificial. Un contratista local legítimo te dará tiempo para revisar el contrato, verificar sus credenciales y comparar presupuestos. Si alguien te presiona para firmar en el momento, retírate.
  7. Dirección física local — no un apartado postal ni un número 1-800
    Busca la dirección comercial en Google Maps. ¿Existe una oficina real allí? ¿Su sitio web tiene un número local de Houston? Los storm chasers de otros estados a menudo operan con un teléfono temporal y un apartado postal.

Señales de Alerta que Debes Evitar

Señal de alerta #1: Ofrecer "pagar tu deducible" o "incluirlo en el seguro."
Según el Código de Seguros de Texas, Sección 707.002, es un delito que un contratista ofrezca pagar o condonar tu deducible de seguro. Esto es fraude de seguros. Si algún roofer hace esta oferta, termina la conversación de inmediato.

Señal de alerta #2: Solo tocan puertas después de tormentas sin presencia empresarial verificable.
Si no pueden mostrarte una dirección local, un COI vigente y referencias locales en 24 horas, no son una empresa confiable para hacer decenas de miles de dólares de trabajo en tu hogar.

Señal de alerta #3: Presupuestos vagos sin especificaciones de materiales.
Un presupuesto profesional debe leer como un alcance de trabajo detallado, no como una cifra escrita en una servilleta. Si no especifica marca, línea de producto, clasificación de viento y garantía, no lo firmes.
